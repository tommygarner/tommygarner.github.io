## Background

In a [previous post](/the-ticketing-industry-blame-game/), I broke down how every player in the ticketing ecosystem, artists, promoters, venues, primary sellers, and secondary markets, is acting rationally within a structure that still produces unfair outcomes for fans. I ended with three directions the industry could move: smarter initial pricing, capped resale exchanges, and better tools to separate real fans from automated buyers.

Noah Kahan's Great Divide Tour, which launched presale in February 2026, tests all three of those directions at once. Kahan sold over one million tickets, including four consecutive nights at Fenway Park described as unprecedented for a non-legacy act. Rather than let secondary market demand absorb the gap between face value and what fans would actually pay, his team built what is likely the most aggressive anti-scalping stack a major touring artist has deployed.

## What He Actually Did

The setup has four layers.

**Face Value Exchange**: Tickets purchased through Ticketmaster can only be resold at the original purchase price, with no additional fees. Tickets are mobile-only and non-transferable between accounts. In the six states where laws prohibit resale price caps (New York, Illinois, Colorado, Virginia, Utah, Connecticut), Ticketmaster says it will still honor face value on its own platform.

**Persona identity verification**: Kahan is the first artist to require some presale participants to complete a selfie check or upload a government ID before accessing tickets. The stated goal is filtering out bot-driven bulk purchases before inventory is released.

**A $100 limited ticket tier**: A specific number of tickets were offered at $100 during presale as a direct affordability measure.

**Mobile-only delivery**: Tickets are released to mobile devices 48 hours before the show, limiting physical transfer paths.[^1]

## What Face Value Exchange Actually Constrains

Face Value Exchange works inside Ticketmaster's ecosystem. Outside of it, it does not.

Tickets that move to StubHub or Vivid Seats through state legal exemptions, workarounds, or unofficial peer transfers can still trade at full market prices. The six states with anti-cap laws are a meaningful gap: New York and Illinois together represent some of the highest-demand concert markets in the country. Ticketmaster saying it honors face value "on its platform" in those states does not prevent a ticket from being relisted elsewhere at three times the original price.

Still, the underlying direction is the right one. Olivia Dean's tour used a similar capped resale framework in 2025. The fact that multiple artists are now opting into this structure suggests it may become a standard offering rather than a one-off arrangement. Whether it becomes the default rather than an opt-in is what would actually move the market.

What Ticketmaster gains from this program is worth naming directly. Artists who use Face Value Exchange push resale activity back inside Ticketmaster's platform. Instead of a secondary transaction going to StubHub or Vivid Seats, it stays with Ticketmaster. The fan advocacy angle and the platform business case point in the same direction here, which is partly why the program exists.

## Persona Slows Bots. It Does Not Stop Them.

Identity verification at presale adds friction, but it adds friction roughly equally for real fans and organized scalping operations.

Bots already use residential proxies and aged accounts that simulate human browsing behavior to get past purchase limits and detection systems. Adding a selfie check or ID upload to a presale signup creates a new hurdle, but the economics of high-demand inventory still justify clearing it. A scalper holding a Fenway ticket bought at face value and reselling it at a significant markup absorbs the overhead of verification easily. Real fans navigating a presale process for the first time feel the friction more directly.

This is the same classification problem I raised in the blame game post. Building better systems that recognize fans from behavioral and identity signals is more promising than building higher fences that sophisticated actors have already learned to climb. Kahan's team is pushing in the right direction, but each new hurdle is also a new problem to solve for the people who do this at scale.

## The $100 Tier Question

The most direct affordability move in the setup is the $100 ticket tier offered during presale. It sold out immediately.

The honest question is who actually purchased it. A $100 ticket in a presale for a sellout stadium tour is exactly the inventory scalping operations are built to capture quickly. Without knowing the purchaser breakdown or whether Kahan's team deployed additional verification specifically for that tier, it is hard to evaluate whether the $100 allocation reached the fans it was designed for or moved into secondary inventory at a premium the moment the sale closed.

A genuine affordability commitment at this demand level looks different: a larger allocation, paired with verification that goes beyond standard presale, and a mechanism to confirm the purchasing fan actually attends. A single price tier that sells in seconds generates goodwill headlines. It does not necessarily put tickets in the hands of the fans it names.

## What He Left on the Table

Kahan could have made more money per ticket. That part of this story rarely gets said plainly.

Artists do not directly profit from secondary market resale. The markup between what a fan paid on primary and what they paid on StubHub goes to the broker and the resale platform, not to the artist. But that gap exists because Kahan priced primary tickets below what the market would bear. If he had used dynamic pricing or simply set higher face values to reflect actual demand, he could have captured some of that consumer surplus himself.

A rough estimate: with one million tickets averaging around $120 at face value, and secondary market data suggesting high-demand sections for a tour of this profile often trade at 50 to 80 percent above face, the consumer surplus that moved to scalpers on a portion of that inventory likely runs into the tens of millions of dollars. If Kahan had captured even part of that through dynamic pricing, the revenue difference would be material.

He chose not to. That is a real financial decision, not a costless one. The fan goodwill tradeoff is legitimate. But it is worth being clear that fan-first pricing at his demand level costs the artist money, and Kahan absorbed that cost deliberately.

## Who Can Actually Use This Model

Face Value Exchange, Persona, and mobile-only delivery require a few things to function: a near-sold-out or sold-out tour, a fan base willing to tolerate friction at presale, and enough demand that the artist makes a real financial sacrifice to cap resale.

An artist with 60 percent sell-through rates has no scalping pressure to fight. Face Value Exchange at that demand level is not a fan protection mechanism because there is no active secondary market to cap. The tools Kahan deployed make sense because his demand significantly outpaces supply. At that scale, the artist has real leverage to dictate terms to the market.

That is the honest scalability limit. The framework Kahan built is a meaningful step forward, and it may push primary platforms toward normalizing capped resale as a standard option. But it is only available to artists who have already solved the demand problem. For mid-tier artists trying to fill seats, the toolkit looks entirely different.
