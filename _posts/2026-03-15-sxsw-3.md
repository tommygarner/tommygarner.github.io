Day three was the most dense day of the week. Three sessions with varying topics that surrounded AI and the music industry superfan.

---

## Session 1: How to Get the Job: Building a Career in Music

Four panelists: Jess Tomlins (artist management, Neon Coast) moderating, with Anthony Langan (Spotify, Global Head of Music and Brand Communications), Farah Thompson (Empire, VP Global Market Strategy), and Nik Ayub (Messina Touring Group, Head of Digital Strategy and Operations). The session was framed around career communication, but the real subject was how relationships actually get built in this industry.

The entry stories set the tone. Langan left banking with no contacts in music and spent four or five months following up on a single Nashville marketing agency role before landing it. His takeaway wasn't about persistence for its own sake, it was about making each follow-up meaningful rather than just a check-in. Ayub went straight from college into Messina and said the differentiator was communicating genuine desire for the role. He used a recent example from the other side: a finalist candidate in a hire made them feel like they were convincing her the job was right for her. That's the wrong dynamic.

The asking-for-help section was the most practically useful part. The consensus was: don't lead with the ask. Demonstrate how you think first. Langan said the people who make lasting impressions offer a specific, substantive reaction to your work before making any request, showing your thinking without being transactional. Ayub flagged that vague asks (grab coffee sometime) create work for the other person. Specific asks (ten minutes to talk about X) are more likely to get a response. Thompson added that silence is not rejection in this industry. People are fielding messages constantly. Follow up, and when someone helps you, close the loop.

The cringe stories were honest and candid. Ayub froze on a deliverable, missed a self-imposed deadline without communicating, and his boss had already told the client it was done. Overcommunicating a delay is always better than a silent miss. Thompson replied-all to an email with a comment about a client who was still CC'd. She called her boss immediately, apologized, and let the boss handle the client call rather than trying to fix it herself. Langan, two weeks into his Nashville job, asked a boardroom full of people about an artist's "debut album." It was their 25th anniversary tour. He has never walked into a room unprepared since.

The reading-the-room conversation was another interesting conversation. Thompson introduced the back-channel tactic: if you and a stakeholder aren't aligned on a large call, don't work it out in front of 30 people. Slack them separately, find the compromise, and come back to the group aligned. She also made the point that knowing when to hold a non-negotiable position matters precisely because most people always compromise. If you're always bulldozing, nobody listens when you actually have a hard line.

The relationship maintenance thread was where Langan said something that stuck. He quoted Jeremy Ehrlich's last day at Spotify: "Don't confuse the king with the throne." The title grants access; the person is what you're actually building a relationship with. He's seen people leave high-profile roles and realize their network was tied to the seat. Thompson said if she realizes she needs to ask someone something and hasn't been in touch, she won't make the ask. She'll just reconnect first. Ayub uses Instagram more than LinkedIn for exactly this reason since it keeps you in someone's life, not just their professional orbit.

## Session 2: Keynote: Why the Future of AI Must Be Human-Centric

![IMG_5873](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/2e885fb0-f410-4b66-99fd-e9164bbdc457)

Dr. Rana el Kaliouby is an AI scientist who pioneered work in emotion AI and affective computing. Her keynote started from a real gap in how AI currently works and built forward from there, rather than starting from fear or hype.

The gap is this: 93% of human communication is nonverbal. Tone, expression, body language. Current AI is almost entirely language-based. That's a massive slice of what humans actually mean when they communicate, and it's not being measured or built for. Rana's framing was sharp: *we only build what we measure for.* There are no benchmarks for the emotional intelligence of AI systems. Until someone builds them, the industry won't optimize for them.

The AI bubble question came up and she handled it well. Her answer was: mostly fiction, some real signals, with a specific concern about companies raising money pre-product and pre-revenue feeding back into the same venture capital ecosystem that funds the cloud compute they buy. Her litmus test for whether an AI company is building something real: what is the net new value created? Not what is the wrapper, not what model is it running on, but what does this do that couldn't be done before?

Two things she said will stick with me. The first was about private data as a moat. If all the models train on the same public data, she argued, their outputs converge. What becomes valuable is protected, proprietary data such health data, behavioral data, IP that isn't in any training set. That framing applies directly to the secondary ticket market data I've been building pipelines around. That data isn't in any model's training set. Its value isn't just that it's useful, it's that it's not shared.

The second was about the ceiling rising along with the floor. AI democratizes creation, which raises the floor for everyone. But it also raises the ceiling because when AI can produce competent work on demand, human originality becomes the premium product, not the baseline. She's optimistic about that shift in a way that felt earned rather than performed.

Her advice for people entering the workforce right now was direct: lean in and try new tools even when they're imperfect. Every workflow is changing. The risk isn't replacement. It's being the person who didn't adapt.

## Session 3: Mentor Session with Sarah Kloboves, Chartmetric

Sarah has been at Chartmetric for nearly four years, leading content and marketing. Chartmetric tracks 13 million artists and 145 million tracks. She led the 2025 Year in Music report as project manager, which involved months of data cleaning, editorial work, and coordination across data, design, and engineering. She's the person who knows what the data can show and which parts of it actually change decisions.

Two things she flagged that I'll carry into project work. First, touring data is the most protected category in music analytics. Chartmetric tracks streaming, social, editorial placements, sync, and brand data, but live event routing decisions are guarded tightly. That gap is real and consequential: it means the market for whoever builds a credible touring data layer is essentially open.

Second, she pointed to tour concentration as a compelling project angle. Why are major artists like Harry Styles doing 30 nights at Madison Square Garden instead of routing broadly? What's driving that decision? Pulling together streaming density by market, secondary ticket velocity, and routing patterns would tell a story that nobody has clean answers to right now.

She also mentioned resources I hadn't heard of: Realcount (described as Chartmetric for tours), Chartmetric's own certification program, and the Measure of Music hackathon as a place to show data work in front of people who hire for exactly these roles. Those went straight onto the list.

## Session 4: The Future of Streaming in the Superfan Era

![IMG_5875](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/5f94ee92-0def-49e3-838f-c4203c4a73a0)

Four panelists from the non-Spotify corner of streaming: Con Raso from Tuned Global, Janisha Jones from Encore Music Tech, Brian Zisook from Audiomack, and Jordan Pettinato from SoundCloud. The session was about how platforms are thinking about superfans, and it clarified something I'd been thinking about loosely.

The vocabulary problem came first. Audience, listeners, followers, fans, and superfans are not interchangeable.
- An audience is people who've been exposed to the music.
- Listeners are passive consumers.
- Followers have opted in but may not be active.
- Fans lean in and seek out the artist.
- Superfans support with both time and money: merch, fan clubs, physical albums, concert tickets in their city.
  
The distinctions matter because the strategies for each tier are completely different, and most platforms collapse them all into a stream count.

Here's a memorable stat from Audiomack: 5% of fans generate more than 75% of revenue for Lil Uzi Vert. Five percent. If you're not identifying that 5% and building experiences specifically for them, you're optimizing for the wrong group.

SoundCloud's approach to surfacing superfans is behavior-based: comments on tracks, DMs between fans and artists, "First Fans" and "Top Fans" designations, and a social graph showing what the artist's own listening history includes. It's a different signal set from what passive listeners generate, and it's the kind of engagement that actually moves recommendation algorithms. Comments, saves, shares, and replays are all inputs. Passive auto-play is not.

The royalty model conversation was the most technically interesting part. Standard pro-rata divides the total pool of streams across rights holders proportionally. Fan-powered royalties, the user-centric model that Audiomack and SoundCloud have both moved toward, route each user's subscription revenue directly to the artists they actually listened to. The difference matters for niche and independent artists with small but deeply engaged fanbases. Under pro-rata, a superfan's streaming behavior benefits whoever has the most total streams on the platform. Under fan-powered royalties, it benefits the artist they actually care about.

The unsolved problem everyone on the panel acknowledged: platform features are converging. When one platform adds vertical video, every other platform copies it. The result is that differentiation erodes. Audiomack and SoundCloud have resisted adding video because it's not who they are and their users don't come for that. The panelist who put it most plainly: everyone's talking about everything except the music.

---

The thread across all three of these sessions was about signal quality. Rana was talking about it in the context of AI: what data matters, what's proprietary, what gets measured. Sarah was talking about it in the context of touring data — what's missing, what would be worth building. The superfan panel was talking about it in terms of engagement signals: the difference between a stream and an intent. Three different framing, same underlying problem: the useful signal is harder to get than the easy signal, and most people are building on the easy signal.
