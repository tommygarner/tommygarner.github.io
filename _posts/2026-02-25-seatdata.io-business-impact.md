The first 12 parts of this series built a demand forecasting system for the secondary ticket market. This post asks the next question: what is acting on those forecasts actually worth?

The answer depends on how the signal gets used. Two mechanisms have the clearest path to revenue: dynamic primary pricing based on secondary velocity, and strategic inventory release timed to demand curves. Both are quantified below using conservative assumptions grounded in published research.

---

## The Revenue Gap

When a primary market ticket is priced below the market-clearing price, the surplus does not disappear. It flows to the secondary market. A concertgoer who buys a $75 face-value ticket and resells it for $150 on StubHub is capturing $75 of value that the artist, venue, or promoter could have retained with better pricing.

This is not a small leak. Research from the Michigan Journal of Economics (2025) found that average secondary market prices for high-demand U.S. concerts and sports events run roughly **2x face value**. At industry scale, 10% of all primary tickets are resold, and 25-30% of inventory for high-demand tours reaches the secondary market within 30 days.

The gap exists because primary pricing teams set prices weeks or months before the event with limited real-time demand data. The secondary market is where that missing demand signal lives.

## What the Research Shows

Dynamic pricing for live events is not theoretical. Multiple published studies have quantified the revenue impact of demand-responsive pricing in adjacent industries.

**Hylen (2022)**, in an MIT thesis on primary market dynamic pricing for the San Antonio Spurs, built a dynamic programming model that aligned ticket prices with real-time demand signals. The conclusion: dynamic pricing demonstrably improves team revenue when prices track actual market conditions rather than fixed pre-season estimates.

The **Journal of Revenue and Pricing Management (2024)** found that MLB teams using variable pricing saw an average revenue increase of **3%** with relatively simple demand models. These were not ML-driven systems. They were rule-based adjustments responding to opponent quality, day of week, and season context. A model with higher predictive accuracy on secondary velocity should capture more.

Outside live events, **Thirumuruganathan et al. (2023)** published results from PREM, an ML-based price-elasticity model deployed across 64.3 million airline flight bookings. The system produced a **37.2% increase in upsell offers accepted** and an estimated **$72.2 million revenue increase** from targeted pricing adjustments.

On the venue side, **Pricefx (2024)** reported that Live Nation's Platinum dynamic pricing program increased premium seat revenue by approximately **70%** since its 2018 launch. Platinum uses real-time demand signals to price a subset of inventory dynamically. The mechanism is exactly what this project's forecasts would inform, extended beyond premium seats to broader inventory.

**Stage Front (2024)** broker research, drawing on the DataVue dataset ($7B in secondary transactions), emphasized a critical distinction: listings only show what is available, not what is selling. The actionable signal is actual sales velocity, which is what this model predicts.

For context on scale: Live Nation reported $22.7 billion in total revenue for 2023, with Ticketmaster generating approximately $3 billion and $311 million in operating profit in 2024, a 32% year-over-year increase. The company manages over 50,000 events globally and sells 620 million tickets annually.

## The Model

The forecasting system is a two-stage pipeline. A classifier (XGBoost) first predicts whether any secondary sales will occur in the next seven days. If the classifier fires (probability above 0.60 threshold), a regressor (MLP) predicts how many tickets will sell.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| RMSE (3-week test window) | **13.37 tickets** |
| Classifier precision (active events) | 76.3% |
| Classifier recall (active events) | 67.6% |
| Precision on dead weeks | 86.7% |
| Recall on dead weeks | 90.9% |
| Test observations | 389,230 |

The classifier is the gatekeeper. When it predicts a dead week, it is right 86.7% of the time. When it fires, 76.3% of flagged events actually have sales. For a pricing team, this means the model generates relatively few false alarms while catching two-thirds of active events.

For low-activity events (under 5 tickets per week), the binary classifier signal is the actionable output: is this event active or dead? For high-activity events (100+ tickets per week), 13.37 RMSE represents roughly 13% relative error, enough precision to distinguish strong momentum from stalling demand.

## Dynamic Pricing

The first revenue mechanism is straightforward. When the model forecasts rising secondary velocity for an event, the primary pricing team has a signal that current face-value prices are below market-clearing. They can hold price on remaining inventory or add a premium before the secondary market absorbs the surplus.

The logic works in both directions. When the model forecasts declining velocity or predicts a dead week, the team can run planned promotional pricing rather than reactive last-minute discounting. A controlled 10% discount at T-14 is a different outcome than a fire sale at T-1.

### Case Study: CFP National Championship

The College Football Playoff National Championship (Indiana vs. Miami) shows how the model reads demand in the final week.

At D-8, the classifier fired with 0.995 probability. Actual sales that week: 655 tickets. At D-7, the model predicted 400 sales against 719 actual. The direction was correct and the magnitude was conservative, exactly the kind of signal a pricing team can act on. If primary prices had been adjusted upward by even 5% in that final week, the incremental revenue on thousands of remaining tickets would be significant.

The model correctly predicted zero sales at event time (clf_prob = 0.0003), confirming it tracks the full lifecycle.

## Inventory Timing

The second revenue mechanism is holding inventory back and releasing it in tranches as demand signals confirm willingness to pay.

A ticket released four weeks before the event at a fixed price may sell for $80. The same ticket released at T-7, after the model confirms strong secondary velocity, could sell for $100 or more. The price appreciation on held inventory is real, but only if the hold decision is backed by a reliable demand signal. Otherwise, held tickets become unsold tickets.

At a 15,000-seat arena with 10% of tickets unsold at current pricing, that is $25 million per year at risk on a 500-event schedule. A 15% reduction in the unsold rate through better timing would save $3.75 million annually.

### Case Study: Super Bowl LX

Super Bowl LX (New England vs. Seattle) is the clearest example of how velocity builds over time.

At D-21, secondary sales were 89 tickets per week. By D-7, velocity had surged to **717 tickets per week**, an 8x increase. The classifier stayed above 0.93 from D-21 through D-2, signaling sustained high demand throughout. A hold-back strategy releasing inventory in tranches as velocity confirmed would have captured the price appreciation across those three weeks.

The model underpredicted absolute volume (214.7 predicted vs. 717 actual at D-7), but the classifier confidence remained high throughout, which is the actionable signal. The direction and intensity were clear even when the point estimate was conservative.

## Segment Opportunity

Not all event categories offer equal pricing opportunity. The gap between primary and secondary market prices varies, and the model's accuracy varies with it.

Concerts show the largest dollar gap. With a secondary premium averaging 120% above face value, the revenue left on the table per ticket is highest in this category. Concerts also represent a large share of total events, making the aggregate opportunity substantial.

Major Sports has the second-largest dollar gap (80% premium on a higher face value), combined with the highest proportion of events predicted active (64.2%). But this category also has the highest RMSE (31.69 tickets), meaning forecasts are less precise and pricing adjustments carry more uncertainty.

Broadway and Comedy have smaller dollar gaps but much lower RMSE (6.46 and 7.08 respectively), making the model's signal highly reliable for these categories. A pricing team could automate adjustments for Broadway and Comedy while requiring human review for Major Sports.

## Scenario Analysis

The revenue model for dynamic pricing uses six parameters. Each is set conservatively in the base case.

| Parameter | Conservative | Moderate | Optimistic | Source |
|-----------|-------------|----------|------------|--------|
| Events/year | 500 | 500 | 500 | Mid-size operator |
| Avg capacity | 10,000 | 10,000 | 10,000 | Industry average |
| Event signal rate | 50% | 60% | 70% | Classifier recall 67.6% |
| Premium inventory | 8% | 10% | 12% | LN Platinum baseline |
| Secondary premium | $35 | $45 | $55 | Below 2x research avg |
| Capture rate | 15% | 25% | 35% | vs. LN Platinum 70%+ |

| Scenario | Per-Event Revenue | Annual (500 events) |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| Conservative | ~$2,100 | **$1.05M** |
| Moderate | ~$6,750 | **$3.38M** |
| Optimistic | ~$16,170 | **$8.09M** |

The conservative case assumes the model only flags half of events (below its actual recall), that only 8% of inventory is eligible for dynamic pricing, and that the team captures just 15% of the available premium. Even under these constraints, the annual value for a 500-event operator is over $1 million.

### Classifier Discrimination

The scenario analysis depends on the classifier correctly distinguishing high-demand from dead events. Two examples from the test set illustrate this discrimination.

**BLACKPINK** consistently showed classifier probabilities above 0.90 across all tracked snapshots, with actual secondary sales of 60-90 tickets per week across multiple venues. The model correctly identified this as sustained mid-tier demand.

**Matilda the Musical** showed classifier probabilities consistently below 0.35, with zero actual secondary sales at every tracked time point. The model correctly classified this as a dead event, meaning no pricing action would be triggered and no false alarm would be generated.

This discrimination is what makes the conservative capture rate defensible. The model does not fire indiscriminately. It reserves high-confidence signals for events where secondary activity confirms real demand.

## At Scale

Live Nation manages over 50,000 events annually and sold 620 million tickets in 2023. Ticketmaster's operating profit was $311 million in 2024.

Scaling the per-event estimates to a 20,000-event portfolio:

| Scenario | Annual Revenue Impact |
|----------|---------------------|
| Conservative | **~$42M** |
| Moderate | **~$135M** |
| Optimistic | **~$323M** |

The conservative estimate of $42 million represents roughly 14% of Ticketmaster's 2024 operating profit. This is a meaningful number, but three caveats apply.

**First**, the model was trained on StubHub data covering a four-month window. Scaling to 50,000+ events across a full year assumes the patterns generalize across seasons and market conditions. A production deployment would need continuous retraining on live data.

**Second**, not all events are dynamically priceable. Artists like Noah Kahan have adopted fan-first fixed pricing strategies that explicitly reject variable pricing. Any deployment must respect artist and label constraints on pricing flexibility.

**Third**, the model treats dynamic pricing and inventory timing as independent mechanisms. In practice they interact: holding inventory back changes the supply signal on the secondary market, which feeds back into the model's inputs. A production system would need to account for this feedback loop.

## Limitations

1. **Secondary market as leading indicator.** The core assumption is that secondary velocity at T-4 to T-6 weeks predicts primary demand. This is supported by Stage Front research but needs a controlled A/B test to validate.

2. **Capture rate of 15-25%.** The conservative range is well below Live Nation Platinum's 70%+ capture rate. It assumes partial adoption and excludes events with weak secondary signal.

3. **Dead inventory assumption.** The 10% unsold rate used in inventory timing calculations varies widely by venue and event type. Venue-level utilization data would sharpen this estimate.

4. **Independence between mechanisms.** Dynamic pricing and inventory release are modeled separately, but in practice they interact. Holding inventory changes secondary market supply signals.

5. **Artist and label constraints.** Dynamic pricing requires artist buy-in. Many artists resist variable pricing for fan-relations reasons, which limits the addressable inventory.

## What Comes Next

The demand forecast is the signal. The pricing engine is the decision layer.

This project built a system that reads secondary market velocity and predicts forward demand. The business impact analysis shows that even conservative use of that signal is worth seven figures annually for a mid-size operator. The next step is a dynamic pricing engine that consumes the 7-day velocity forecasts in real time, maps them to demand curves, and generates daily pricing recommendations. That engine, combined with A/B validation at a venue or promoter level, would close the loop between prediction and action.
