## Abstract

Part 6 delivered 18.98 RMSE using RandomizedSearchCV on XGBoost. I had a working model, but it still felt unfinished. My next move was to try to optimize hyperparameters even further.

I spent 400 trials and 14 hours of compute time pushing that number to 18.53 RMSE, a 2.35% improvement. This wasn't much, prompting me to reconsider feature engineering and additions hiding in secondary market timing patterns, but first I had to learn the hard way that these small gains were not worth it.

## The Logical Next Step

Coming off Part 6, I wanted to discover the most optimal hyperparameters to push performance. The RandomizedSearchCV approach simply tested hardcoded, random combinations without learning from previous trials. XGBoost had far more parameters that interact with the model.

I already had a strong baseline at 18.98 RMSE. However, I recently learned of Optuna as a standard for Bayesian optimization and wanted to give it a shot with my data. What I didn't realize was that I was about to spend considerable time and compute resources on diminishing returns.

---

## Optuna Over RandomizedSearchCV

The gap between RandomizedSearchCV and modern optimization was obvious. Random search tests independent trials without learning from previous results. If trial 50 found that `learning_rate=0.05` works well, trial 51 would test `learning_rate=0.15` with equal probability, no memory, and no strategy.

### Bayesian Optimization

Optuna implements **Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE)**, a Bayesian optimization algorithm that builds a probabilistic model of parameter performance.

<img width="810" height="540" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/2df9db85-a5e2-40c2-ba3a-95ed1f5a393a" />

*Figure 2: Visualization of TPE for 2-dimensional hyperparameter tuning. Image by Alexander Elvers via Wikipedia Commons*

Optuna remembers two distributions:
1. Good parameters containing the top 25% of trials
2. Poor parameters containing the remaining 75%

Each new trial samples from regions where good parameters are most concentrated relative to poor ones. This results in Optuna typically finding optimal configurations in 50-70% fewer trials than even random search.

<img width="1315" height="544" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/696d7d1d-dcf1-4aef-b29e-183d564c05c6" />

*Figure 3: Comparing Grid Search, Random Search, and Optuna strategies for finding optimal hyperparameter configurations*


For my XGBoost optimization, this meant purposeful exploration of learning rates, tree depths, and regularization parameters to optimize these hyperparameters and squeeze out any performance from an already solid baseline.

## Experimenting with Optuna

I designed the search around XGBoost's core parameters, informed from previous modeling posts:

**Learning and Regularization:**
- Learning rate: 0.01-0.3
- L1/L2 regularization: 0.0-3.0
- Minimum child weight: 1-20

**Tree Makeup:**
- Max depth: 3-15
- Subsample ratios: 0.6-1.0

My plan was to then run 200 trials for the classifier and 200 trials for the regressor, plus exploring some threshold optimizations. What I expected was steady improvement across trials, but what I got instead was diminishing returns.

### Threshold Optimization

One of the most valuable insights from Optuna's search was identifying that the **classification threshold**, often defaulted to 0.50, is a critical hyperparameter deserving systematic optimization.

This optimization found that a threshold of 0.90 minimized RMSE in ticket space. This simple change reduced false positives, requiring my model be 90% confident or higher when classifying events to have a sale.

---

> ### Metric Transparency
>
> The **18.53 RMSE** above is the **full-test-set RMSE** evaluated across all 58,022 test events, including the ~71% of snapshots with zero sales during the final two-week window. Because predicting zero for a zero-sale event is correct and easy, including these rows lowers the aggregate error significantly.
>
> There are actually three ways to slice this metric, each answering a different question:
>
> | Label | Subset | Notes |
> |-------|--------|-------|
> | **Full-test-set RMSE** | All 58,022 events | The 18.53 figure deployed-system accuracy |
> | **Active-event RMSE** | ~16,900 events with confirmed sales | Harder metric that penalizes missed events |
> | **Predicted-positive RMSE** | Events the model chose to predict | Best-case view that excludes all missed events |
>
> The 18.53 figure is still valid. It reflects deployed-system performance in a market where most events have zero sales in the final two weeks. Active-event RMSE is the harder, more informative benchmark for revenue forecasting use cases.
>
> For comparison: the unified baseline model from Part 8 (Segmentation) reports **30.73 RMSE**, but on active-positive rows only (threshold 0.50, non-Optuna models). That's a different denominator AND a weaker model.

## Lessons I Learned

After 400 trials and 14 hours of compute, the technicality of the experiment was great as a way to learn all about Optuna, but the more important takeaway was about resource allocation and knowing when to pivot.

### Effort to Impact

From this experiment, I saw:

- 0.445 tickets RMSE decrease (2.35% improvement)
- The best results were found around trial 120 for both the clf and reg models
- The final 160 trials showed minimal improvement
- And only 2-3 parameters drove 90% of variation

Both models had rapid early gains of exploration, focused imrovement, and incremental gains that barely moved performance. I was chasing increasingly smaller improvements.

### When Hyperparameter Optimization Might Pay Off

**High-Impact Scenarios:**
1. New model architectures with unknown parameter interactions
2. Production systems where that 1-2% translate to real business value
3. Early in modeling trying to get a lay of the land
4. Even larger search spaces

### My Case in Retrospect

My case checked many boxes for successful optimization: 9 hyperparameters, expensive objective functions, production forecasting system.

Optuna was technically the right choice, but the question was whether optimization was the right priority.

The intelligent search worked exactly as designed, finding better configurations 40% faster than random search would've. But I was crazy enough to run these fully to completion for 14 hours overnight when the signal to stop was clear by trial 120.

--- 

## Conclusion

Starting from Part 6's **18.98 RMSE** (manual RandomizedSearchCV), I adopted Optuna to systematically explore 400 trials across XGBoost architectures.

**Key Results:**
- **Final RMSE:** 18.535 tickets
- **Improvement:** 0.445 tickets (2.35% over Part 6)
- **Compute time:** 14 hours (6 parallel workers)
- **Trial efficiency:** Found optimal config by trial 120 (Bayesian search beat random search)

With a solid baseline established, the path forward wasn't more optimization, but would become questioning which signals I was missing entirely.
