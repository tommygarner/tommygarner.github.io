Every model in this series treats events as anonymous. The features describe market state: active listings, get-in price, days to event, venue capacity. None of them say anything about who is performing.

That felt like a gap worth closing. Two concerts with identical market snapshots might behave very differently if one is Taylor Swift and the other is a regional act at a 500-seat club. The model has no way to distinguish them today.

So I built a pipeline to inject artist and team identity through entity embeddings: Wikipedia and Last.fm text, converted to dense vectors by a sentence transformer and compressed with PCA, then joined onto the training data as additional features. The ablation ran 32 model configurations overnight. None of the embedding configs beat the tabular baseline.

The reason why is the more useful finding: the secondary market already encodes who the artist is. A $300 get-in price and 15 active listings at a 20,000-seat venue is not just a market condition. It is Taylor Swift. The tabular features are identity-encoded-at-prediction-time. Adding a Wikipedia embedding adds nothing the listing price hasn't already said.

Sports is the exception, and understanding why tells you something about where external signals actually help.

---

## The Hypothesis

A tight market and a loose market tell you supply-demand balance, but not why that balance exists. Two shows with identical listing counts and get-in prices resolve differently as the event approaches if one has an insatiable fanbase and the other has an overpriced mid-tier lineup.

Embeddings offer a way to give the model identity without hand-engineering artist-level features. Rather than building explicit flags like "is this a top-40 act" or "does this team have a winning record," you represent each entity as a dense vector derived from text that describes them. The model can learn which dimensions of that vector carry signal for predicting sales.

This borrows the cold-start framing from recommendation systems: when behavioral history is sparse or absent, use content-based features to bootstrap signal. The question was whether that applies to a market already observed through listing data.

## Building the Pipeline

### Slugs

The first problem is matching event records to external sources. Each training row has an `event_name` like "Taylor Swift: The Eras Tour" or "Los Angeles Lakers vs Boston Celtics." To look these up in Wikipedia or Last.fm, I need a stable normalized identifier.

Slug derivation differs by event type. Concert and comedy names get stripped of venue info ("at Madison Square Garden"), feature tags ("presented by", "with special guest"), and format markers ("VIP Package"), then normalized to lowercase underscores: `artist::taylor_swift`. Sports events normalize the full matchup: `team::unknown::los_angeles_lakers_vs_boston_celtics`. Everything else normalizes the full name: `event::hamilton`.

The normalization is lenient enough that "O.A.R." and "OAR" map to the same slug, but strict enough that "Taylor Swift" and "Taylor Swift - VIP" both reduce to `artist::taylor_swift`. Keeping this logic identical across the collection script, enrichment script, and join step matters — any mismatch becomes zero coverage.

### Wikipedia and Last.fm

Wikipedia is the primary source. For each unique entity slug, the enrichment script pulls up to 2,000 characters of summary from the Wikipedia API. When an artist name returns a disambiguation page, the script tries each option until one resolves to an actual article.

*Figure 2: Wikipedia data fetched for the San Antonio Spurs*

Artist Wikipedia coverage comes out at about 61%. Teams hit 95% because major and minor league names resolve cleanly. Venues land at 69%, other events at 81%. The artist gap is expected: well-known touring acts have pages, regional and emerging acts often don't.

For the 1,299 concert and comedy artists with no Wikipedia page, I used the Last.fm API. The `artist.getInfo` endpoint returns a bio, genre tags, and a listener count, combined into a single text string:

```python
def _build_text(row) -> str:
    name  = row.get('lastfm_name') or row['entity_name']
    parts = [str(name)]
    if row.get('tags'):
        parts.append(f"Genres: {row['tags']}")
    if row.get('bio'):
        parts.append(str(row['bio']))
    if row.get('listeners', 0) > 0:
        parts.append(f"Last.fm listeners: {int(row['listeners']):,}")
    return '. '.join(p for p in parts if p)
```

The resulting string for O.A.R. looks like: "O.A.R.. Genres: rock, alternative, jam band, chill. O.A.R. (Of A Revolution) is an alternative rock band which formed in Rockville, Maryland...Last.fm listeners: 585,797." That string gets passed through the sentence transformer alongside Wikipedia summaries for other artists.

### Sentence Transformer and PCA

All text runs through `all-MiniLM-L6-v2`, a distilled BERT model fine-tuned to produce semantically coherent sentence embeddings. It outputs 384-dimensional vectors: two pieces of text describing similar things end up close together in that space, unrelated descriptions land far apart. It is fast enough to encode 18,000 entities in a few minutes on CPU.

<img width="1200" height="630" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/69662ceb-3f9a-4b67-87e0-449a38cd5274" />

*Figure 4: all-MiniLM-L6-v2 from HuggingFace*

The result is an 18,104 by 384 matrix. Feeding all 384 dimensions directly into a tree model alongside 29 tabular features would introduce far more noise than signal, so I apply PCA after standardizing the embedding matrix:

```python
scaler  = StandardScaler()
scaled  = scaler.fit_transform(raw_embeddings)

pca     = PCA(n_components=n_components, random_state=42)
reduced = pca.fit_transform(scaled).astype(np.float32)
```

A secondary PCA pass on the joined training data finds 102 components explaining at least 75% of variance. The final entity store contains 28,598 rows at 169 dimensions, compressed to 102 for model input.

### Coverage

After joining on slug, about 68% of train and validation rows match an entity in the store. Test coverage drops to 61.9% because the held-out window contains some events not present during enrichment. Unmatched rows receive zero vectors.

Coverage is not uniform. Concerts match at 87.2%, Major and Minor Sports near 99%, Broadway and Theater at just 7.6%. That last number matters: when 92% of Broadway rows carry zero vectors, any embedding config behaves almost identically to the baseline for that bucket.

## The Ablation

The evaluation is a feature ablation: does adding embeddings to the baseline improve the two-stage pipeline?

Four feature configs: baseline (29 tabular features only), then baseline plus the first 10, 50, or 102 PCA components. Four model families: XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, and sklearn MLP. Both stages: classifier and regressor. That is 32 training slots, each running 60 Optuna trials with a 15-minute timeout. Training data is 5.1M rows; validation is 1.04M rows.

The comparison metric is whether any embedding config beats the 18.53 RMSE champion from Part 7.

---

## Results

None of them do. At 60 Optuna trials, the baseline scores 19.09 RMSE. Adding 10 PCA components adds 0.15. Adding 50 adds 0.22. Adding all 102 adds 0.12. Every embedding configuration makes the pipeline slightly worse.

The classifier stage tells a marginally different story. PR-AUC improves from 0.814 (baseline) to 0.819 with emb10 or emb50. Embeddings give the classifier a small edge in identifying which events will have nonzero sales. But that edge does not survive the pipeline. Better zero-sale classification does not translate into better volume predictions on events that actually sell.

Restricting evaluation to the 35,936 rows with actual (non-zero) embeddings removes the noise from unmatched rows. emb200 comes within 0.02 RMSE of the baseline on matched rows, essentially a tie. The case against embeddings holds even after the zero-vector distortion is removed.

### Where the Hypothesis Partially Holds

The bucket breakdown is where this gets interesting. Sports events benefit from embeddings. Minor and Other Sports improves by 0.77 RMSE with emb200. Major Sports improves by 0.36. The gains are modest but consistent across both segments.

**Concerts go the other direction.** Despite 87.2% match coverage, adding artist embeddings increases concert RMSE by 0.93. Genre history, career arc, critical reception — none of it helps predict how many tickets sell in the next seven days.

## Why the Market Already Knows the Artist

The tabular features are not identity-agnostic. They are identity-encoded-at-prediction-time.

A $300 get-in price and 15 active listings at a 20,000-seat venue does not just describe supply and demand. It describes Taylor Swift. A $25 get-in price and 400 active listings at a 2,000-seat club describes a mid-tier regional act. The model already learns to distinguish those events because the market has priced in identity through current conditions. Adding a 384-dimensional vector summarizing Taylor Swift's Wikipedia page tells the model nothing it could not already infer from the listing price.

For concerts, market state is a better proxy for artist demand than a text description of that artist. The text describes what an artist is. The listing data describes how their specific show is performing right now. Seven-day sales velocity cares about the second.

Sports is the partial exception. Two teams at similar listing counts and get-in prices might differ in ways the tabular features cannot fully capture: whether one franchise draws nationally, whether the matchup has playoff implications, whether the series is tied 3-3. A PCA-compressed Wikipedia vector of "Los Angeles Lakers" carries something the listing data cannot derive from inventory counts alone — franchise market size, rivalry context, brand weight. The sports result is small but consistent.

## A Side Output: Dashboard Context Cards

A secondary output of the enrichment pipeline is 13,804 plain-English entity descriptions, generated via GPT-4o-mini with Gemini Flash as a fallback. These are 2-3 sentence summaries used in the Event Explorer dashboard, where a pricing manager can click on any event and see a brief description of the artist, venue, or team. They are stored in BigQuery as `dim_entity_summaries`. Total generation cost was $1.92 across 7.4M input tokens and 1.3M output tokens. These summaries never enter the training pipeline.

## What Comes Next

Part 9's failure is instructive: static text descriptions of identity did not help because the market already encodes identity through price. The natural follow-up is whether *time-varying* signals do better.

Not another Wikipedia scrape. Instead: Spotify popularity updated weekly, Last.fm listener counts, team win-loss records keyed to the snapshot date, Reddit sentiment from sport and music subreddits. These are causal and proximate in a way that a career biography is not. If an artist is trending this week or a team just won five straight, that information should show up in listing behavior within days. The question is whether adding it explicitly gives the model signal it doesn't already have from the lagged tabular features.

Part 10 runs that test.
