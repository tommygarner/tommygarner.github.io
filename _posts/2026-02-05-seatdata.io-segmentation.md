## Overview

A Beyoncé concert and Lakers play-in game seem fundamentally different. Shouldn't they need different prediction models? My intuition says yes. Concert demand spikes with social buzz, sports demand follows team performance. Comedy shows have different pricing patterns than NBA games. **Logic suggests exploring specialized models for each category.**

The unified model from earlier posts treats all events identically, learning the patterns of segments from dummy codings. But what if training separate models for concerts, sports, theater and comedy would capture the unique demand patterns of each category? **The business case seemed obvious: let each model become an expert in its field.**

I trained segment-specific models for my 7 event categories and compared them against the unified approach. **The initial results looked like what I'd hypothesized, but a closer look told a different story.**

---

## Setup

### The Experiment

Using my existing two-stage XGBoost pipeline (classify if sales occur, then predict how many), I trained separate models for each event category and compared their performance against the unified model on events that actually sold tickets. These were the hardest cases where the model has to predict a nonzero amount.

### My Categories

| Segment | Train Rows | Test Rows |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Concert | 1,903,137 | 15,818 |
| Broadway & Theater | 1,383,917 | 18,045 |
| Other | 663,217 | 9,225 |
| Major Sports | 543,514 | 5,909 |
| Comedy | 463,750 | 6,272 |
| Minor/Other Sports | 139,755 | 2,596 |
| Festivals | 17,223 | 157 |

**Clearly some categories have way more data than others, which would become an important detail later on.**

### Evaluation

I measured prediction error on **events that actually sold tickets**, much harder cases than finding the (many) zeros. **Revenue forecasting happens on the events that sell.** These nearly **17K events out of 58K total test cases** represent where pricing decision actually matter.

The metric is end-to-end RMSE in tickets. This is how far the model's predictions were from actual sales volume.

## Bootstrap Tests

To check whether these improvements are real or just noise, I ran bootstrap significance tests with 1000 resamples per segment. I report the 95% confidence interval for the RMSE difference and a one-tailed p-value.

<img width="1787" height="594" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/4c185556-121c-4163-998c-93d779f6629e" />

*Figure 3: Bootstrap 95% CI and distributions for RMSE improvement by segment*

| Segment | RMSE Improvement | 95% CI | p-value | Significant? |
|---------|-----------------|--------|---------|--------------|
| Comedy | +1.57 tickets | [+0.94, +2.26] | 0.000 | Yes (***) |
| Major Sports | +0.88 tickets | [-0.12, +1.84] | 0.040 | Marginal (**) |
| Festivals | +0.73 tickets | [-2.42, +4.17] | 0.321 | No |
| Broadway & Theater | +0.30 tickets | [-0.87, +1.24] | 0.285 | No |
| Concert | +0.00 tickets | [-0.63, +0.65] | 0.507 | No |
| Minor/Other Sports | -0.32 tickets | [-1.07, +0.35] | 0.808 | No |
| Other | -0.85 tickets | [-2.42, +0.76] | 0.836 | No |

Bootstrapping allowed me to sample with replacement of my data and predictions to get a more holistic view on distributions. **Only Comedy shows a statistically significant improvement** with a CI that excludes zero. Major Sports is marginal. **The results painted a different picture than the initial test-set improvements suggested.**

---

## Cross-Validation

**Cross-validation revealed the definitive answer.** 

The test set is one specific time window. To see whether segmentation holds up across different time periods, I ran 3-fold time-based cross-validation, training on earlier data and evaluating predictions on the final 14 days of each fold.

| Segment | Unified CV RMSE | Segment CV RMSE | Result |
|---------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| Broadway & Theater | 7.64 ± 1.74 | 8.78 ± 0.95 | unified wins |
| Comedy | 10.62 ± 5.23 | 11.86 ± 5.86 | unified wins |
| Concert | 12.38 ± 1.27 | 12.63 ± 1.29 | tied |
| Festivals | 8.20 ± 4.65 | 14.33 ± 10.25 | unified wins |
| Major Sports | 15.28 ± 2.60 | 15.52 ± 2.49 | tied |
| Minor/Other Sports | 11.07 ± 0.79 | 11.91 ± 3.79 | unified wins |
| Other | 10.20 ± 0.10 | 10.96 ± 1.43 | unified wins |

**In every segment, the unified model wins or ties.** This directly contradicts the test-set results. **Festivals is the starkest reversal: a +0.73 test-set improvement collapses to a -6.13 CV gap**, a clear sign of overfitting on a 17K-row dataset.

**The segment models were picking up on patterns specific to the test period, not stable features of these categories.**

---

### The Case for Comedy

Comedy seemed like **the only success story for this experiment** with statistically significant improvements and clean confidence intervals. Feature importance analysis showed that **Comedy had the lowest divergence from the unified model**, meaning that it wasn't learning different signals, it was just calibrated differently on more homogeneous data.

But even here, **cross-validation revealed that the unified model's access to more diverse training data won out.** The Comedy segment model's **16% test-set improvement became a 1.24 ticket loss in cross-validation.**

## Missing Features in Major Sports

Major Sports shows **the other side of the data problem** that wasn't just volume but **missing features entirely**. High-stakes games like the playoffs and rivalry games have sales volumes that no current feature captures. **Without team standings, playoff probability, or opponent quality, the model predicts blindly** for the most important evnets.

**A dedicated Major Sports model with the same incomplete features won't fix this. No amount of segmentation fixes incomplete data.**

---

## Data Volume Beats Specialization

**The core issue is that segmentation introduces more parameters to fit.** With limited data, especially for smaller categories like Festivals and Minor/Other Sports, the segment models cannot generalize beyond the training window. **A unified model benefits from seeing all events and learns more stable patterns.**

Comedy and Major Sports illustrate this from different angles. **Comedy had the most promising test results but still lost to the unified model's broader training exposure** to data in cross-validation. **Major Sports showed that, even with dedicated models, missing features create a blindness for predictions that specialization can't solve.**

So, though the business intuition seemed right, **cross-validation proved my hypothesis wrong.** This experience taught me that **more training data usually beats specialization when data is limited.** Access to **5M+ training examples across all categories** gave the unified model more stable signal than any single category could provide.

**Segmented models would need significantly more data per category and more time periods** to reliably outperform this unified approach.

---

## Conclusion

**This experiment humbled my assumptions about specialization in machine learning.** Every business instinct suggested that concert demand is fundamentally different than sports demand and requires specialized models. Though the initial test results seemed to confirm this intuition for Comedy, **validation told a different story.**

**Cross-validation showed that improvements were not generalizable across different time periods.** The unified model's access to **5+ million diverse training examples consistently outperformed segment models** trained on narrower datasets.

The lesson extends beyond model architecture to data strategy. **Major Sports showed that missing features (team standings, playoff context) create a prediction blindness that no amount of specialization can fix.** Comedy showed that **even statistically significant improvements can collapse under time-related validation.**

**The path forward would then become better features, driving the next phase of this project.**
