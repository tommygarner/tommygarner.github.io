Day two split between sessions and the demo floor. The Spotify masterclass was the most practically dense hour of the week, although I have never seen the S4A backend. The sports innovation panel that followed was an exciting time seeing the data applications from a new industry.

## Session 1: Spotify for Artists Masterclass

![IMG_5856](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/2ab8a90a-b1f8-4d04-a4fa-a61cf0a33760)

Three people from Spotify walked through the full toolkit available to artists and their teams: Miller Guth (Artist and Label Partnerships), Talia Kraines (Editorial and Playlist Strategy), and Chris Demas (Go to Market and Commercial Partnerships). This was less of a panel and more of a structured product walkthrough, which made it pretty dense.

The opening numbers set the context: 751 million active users, 290 million paying subscribers, 184 markets, $70 billion paid out to rights holders to date, and 100,000+ songs released every single day. That last number kept coming back all week from different angles. It's the defining constraint of the streaming era, and every strategy conversation eventually runs into it.

**The release cycle.** The session structured everything around the pre-release, release, and post-release phases. The pre-release tools were the most interesting to me because they're predictive. Countdown pages are Spotify's native pre-save solution. Artists who add a clip to their countdown generate 2x the presaves compared to artists who don't. Also, 70% of presavers stream the album in the first week. That is a high-intent signal. If 70% of the people who presave show up on release day, presave volume is a leading indicator of launch demand, not just a marketing metric. Nobody is building that into forecasting models yet, and they should be. These stats also showed me that lift associations are a really useful application when it comes to the industry!

**Clips.** Spotify's short-form layer. Not a TikTok clone, since the use cases are specific. Tease an upcoming release. Promote a pre-release single. Push listeners deeper into your existing catalog. You can schedule a clip to drop on release day. Because clips live inside the listening experience rather than a separate feed, they reach people who are already in music consumption mode, which is a meaningfully different context than social media.

**Editorial and Fresh Finds.** Talia covered this and it was the most valuable section for anyone managing an independent artist. Fresh Finds is Spotify's discovery program for new indie artists. Per their Loud & Clear report, 1 in 10 artists placed on Fresh Finds go on to have meaningful careers. Two case studies made the timeline concrete: Mercer Henderson went from her fourth single landing on Fresh Finds in February 2023, to New Music Friday in November of that year, to Fresh Finds cover in 2024, and is now at 610K monthly listeners. Ella Langley also went from Fresh Finds Country in 2021 to her first Hot Country cover in 2024. Three years in each case. The lesson isn't that Fresh Finds is a shortcut. It's that editorial discovery compounds over time when there's catalog underneath it.

Pitching is done through Spotify for Artists before release. You describe the music and the release plan. There's a character limit, which forces specificity. The editorial team reviews pitches separately from the algorithmic placement, so getting editorial placement requires making a case, it doesn't happen automatically from algorithmic performance.

**The Drop** was new to me. It's a weekly visual feed where editors annotate new tracks with context before they surface. It targets younger users and self-described music superfans. The human editorial layer is what differentiates it from the algorithmic feed. It's closer to a curated magazine inside the app than a recommendations engine.

**Paid tools.** Marquee drives a 3x lift in listeners. Showcase drives 5x more active streams and makes listeners 3x more likely to save. Both are recommended to deploy in the 21 days after release and targeted at fans and near-fans, not cold audiences. 

**What I'll keep thinking about.** Two things. SongDNA was a feature that acts as a network graph mapping the people behind a song: writers, producers, featured artists, all in a visual web. Spotify already built this inside their platform. I've been thinking about building something similar at the genre or scene levelL who produces for whom, which writers cluster together, which managers connect to which labels. 

The second thing is Fans First, Spotify's program that routes presale ticket access to artists' top fans via DTC email. That's a closed loop: engagement data identifies superfans, superfans get early access, early access deepens the relationship. Fans First gets at it from the supply side.

## Accenture Demo Floor

From there, I made my way over to the Accenture office and found three demos on the first floor. 

The Disney asset search booth was building something I hadn't seen framed quite this way: a semantic search system for video assets that uses vector embeddings and requires no training data. The constraint is what makes it interesting: the system has to work out of the box on new projects without fine-tuning. Assets go into GitHub, run through a vectorization pipeline, and get scored against queries. It's a content discovery problem that looks like a recommendation problem. The same methodology maps directly to anything where you have unstructured media and need to surface relevant matches quickly.

The PAM navigation demo was the most directly relevant to live events. Arena wayfinding with food and beverage surfaced along your route is the surface feature. The interesting layer underneath is preconfigured contingency planning: the system knows that a rain delay at a PGA Tour event changes the venue configuration, and it adapts the navigation accordingly. Most venue apps are static maps. This one is operational state-aware.

![IMG_5859](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/697e33eb-e8c5-4cd7-8f5a-6f2801c4587f)

The agentic commerce demo was Accenture's answer to the AEO (answer engine optimization) problem: getting products discovered in AI search interfaces rather than traditional search. The framing was nearly identical to a company I recently came across called OneLive and what they have built for music e-commerce. Seeing it applied to retail at this scale made the music application feel more validated.

## Session 2: When Innovation Meets Competition — The New Era of Global Sports

![IMG_5862](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/3a3ded37-5026-4c27-a9ed-c22d8792a2e6)

Kirk Goldsberry from UT Austin's Business of Sports Institute is someone I've followed for quite some time, even taking the undergraduate course from his colleage Joshua Fink. Kevin Lopes from ESPN and RJ Paige from the NFL also joined the panel. The session was all about sports technology, but the real thread running through it was the same one from the Spotify masterclass: the data is there, and most organizations are still not using it well.

The technology layer has gotten super specific. Camera systems now track 29 points of the human body at high frequency. Hawkeye handles officiating accuracy and line calls in tennis. SkeleTrack processes skeletal data for player safety and broadcast animation. The NFL and ESPN are running dual-point body tracking that produces broadcast overlays more realistic than anything that existed five years ago. 

The governance question was more complicated than the technology. What you can do with pose data looks different depending on whether it's going to the betting market or the broadcast and media side. College sports are investing in the same infrastructure now, and nobody has cleanly answered whether college-level body tracking data can flow to professional clubs the same way league-wide data is distributed across teams.

Kirk's historical framing was useful: Moneyball in 2003 started a 23-year run that put baseball ahead analytically. F1 is now the most data-sophisticated sport in the world with terabytes per race. Everyone else is catching up on different timelines.

The five-year horizon conversation was what I found most useful. 
- Kevin's bet: GenAI transforms advertising first. Hypertargeted CPMs, video generation for short-form ads customized by audience segment.
- RJ's angle: the NFL plays international games and the question isn't whether people in Melbourne care about the game, it's whether the digital infrastructure is ready to capture that interest and eventually develop talent from those markets.
- Kirk's: live events become more valuable as AI generates more of the digital world.

The health optimization thread was also very interesting. The panel framed it at three levels: league, team, and player. The team-level motivation is purely self-interested: keep players on the field. The player-level question is more interesting: Victor Wembanyama treating his body as a serious long-term investment, or the open research question around why women tear ACLs at significantly higher rates than men. The framing the panel used was moving from reactive health management to preventative. That's the same shift the whole analytics conversation is about, just applied to bodies instead of revenue.

---

The through-line between the Spotify masterclass and the sports panel was clearer by the end of the day than it had been in the morning. Both conversations were about the gap between data that exists and decisions that data should be driving. Spotify has built tools that let artists act on the signal, but most don't use them. Sports organizations have body-tracking infrastructure that produces terabytes per game, yet most teams are still working reactively. The floor is low in both cases, which means showing up with actual data literacy is still a differentiator.
