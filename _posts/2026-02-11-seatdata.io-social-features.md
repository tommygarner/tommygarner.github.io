## Overview

Part 9 tested whether Wikipedia entity embeddings improve ticket sales forecasting. The result was negative: none of the embedding configurations improved on the tabular baseline, and the concert bucket got worse by nearly 1 RMSE point. The explanation is that tabular market features already encode identity indirectly. A $300 get-in price and 15 active listings in a 20,000-seat arena tells you more about likely demand than knowing an artist's Wikipedia bio.

But there is a meaningful difference between two types of identity signal:

- **Static identity:** What this artist or team is (career history, genre, franchise market size)
- **Time-varying momentum:** How this artist or team is performing *right now* (streaming activity this week, current win streak, recent news coverage)

Wikipedia embeddings capture the first type. They are fixed at collection time and do not change with the market. The second type is different. If a team has won 8 of its last 10 games, demand for their next home game is likely higher than it was two months ago when they were .500. If an artist's Spotify popularity jumped 15 points since their last tour, the demand curve for this run should look different than the model would expect from historical patterns alone.

This post documents the collection of three time-varying signals: Last.fm listener counts, Spotify artist popularity, and team W/L records, plus a fourth that was considered and skipped. These signals feed into the Part 11 training sweep.

## Signal Architecture

Each signal has a different update frequency, join key, and source API. The diagram below shows how they flow from raw APIs to the parquet caches used in training.

<img width="6381" height="2340" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/09c554e6-5602-4f79-a956-adad2f200f79" />

*Figure 1: API architecture to join more features*


The join key differs by signal type:

| Signal | Join Key | Frequency |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Last.fm listeners | `slug` (static: one fetch per artist) | Static at collection time |
| Spotify popularity/followers | `slug` (static: one fetch per artist) | Static at collection time |
| Team records | `slug + snapshot_date` (PIT: one row per team per day) | Daily |

Last.fm and Spotify are fetched once per artist and treated as static for training. Team records are fetched per team per date, making them genuinely point-in-time.

## Last.fm Listener Counts

Last.fm exposes a monthly listener count for each artist through the `artist.getInfo` endpoint. This is different from the embeddings experiment. In Part 9, we used Last.fm bios and tags as text input for sentence transformers. Here we use the numeric listener count directly as a feature.

The listener count is a measure of current audience size, not historical fame. It updates as listeners add and remove plays. An artist with 2 million monthly listeners today is drawing more active attention than one with 200,000, regardless of their Wikipedia page length or Grammy history.

To collect this data from Last.fm, I ran a similar Python script that now iterates through all unique artist slugs in the training data and calls the API. I also checkpointed results every 50 artists in case my computer ran OOM and lost all progress, especially considering a rate limit.

**Coverage:**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total slugs attempted | ~6,800 |
| Found | ~2,875 |
| Found rate | 45.2% |
| Feature | `lastfm_listeners` |

The 45% found rate reflects how the slug system works: only named artists with pages on Last.fm match. Broadway shows, comedy events, and sports games do not have Last.fm pages, so those rows get `NULL` for this feature. Concert and Comedy events see the highest match rates.

## Spotify Popularity and Followers

Spotify returns two numeric signals per artist: `followers_total` (cumulative subscriber count) and `popularity` (a 0–100 score Spotify recomputes weekly based on recent streaming activity).

<img width="664" height="586" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f43fa0ae-547c-4353-af85-109d5bd23c66" />

*Figure 2: Spotify API architecture*


The `popularity` score is the more interesting of the two. Unlike follower count, which mostly reflects an artist's historical peak, popularity is recency-weighted. A breakout artist whose plays doubled in the past month will score higher than an established artist who has not released anything recently and is seeing natural listener decay. This is the "hot right now" signal that follower count cannot capture.

Similar to the Last.fm Python script, I also used artist slugs classified under Concert and Comedy only to search for these two Spotify signals. Sports teams, Broadway shows, and festival events are not meaningful Spotify lookups.

**Coverage:**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total rows | ~7,408 |
| Found rate | 92.8% |
| Categories covered | Concert, Comedy |
| Features | `spotify_popularity`, `spotify_followers` |

The 92.8% found rate is significantly higher than Last.fm because Spotify's search endpoint is more permissive and more major-label artists have Spotify pages.

**Data quality filter:** A `spotify_followers < 500` threshold was applied after collection. Some slug matches returned a coincidentally named act with a handful of followers rather than the intended touring artist. A slug like `artist::peach_bowl` could match a small local band rather than returning no result. Setting a floor at 500 followers sets those spurious matches to `NULL` rather than feeding incorrect signals into the model.

## Team Records: Point-in-Time W/L Data

For the sports segments (Major Sports and Minor Sports), the relevant signal is the team's current season standing at the time of the snapshot. A game between two teams with 60% win rates should see different demand than the same matchup when both teams are 30%.

The challenge is that this has to be point-in-time. If a snapshot is from January 10th, the team record should reflect the season through January 9th, not the final season record. Using the final record would leak future information into the training data.

So, I decided to use several APIs to collect this W/L data from a couple of major leagues: 
- NBA: `nba_api` library, `TeamGameLog` endpoint
- NFL: `nfl_data_py` library
- MLB: MLB StatsAPI

<img width="1600" height="1109" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/d9ade3cf-6ed4-4f6e-9835-da2ba89cf810" />

*Figure 3: NBA API library architecture*

For each team, the script builds a daily record table: wins, losses, win percentage, and current streak (positive = win streak length, negative = losing streak) up to the cutoff date. The result is one row per team per date.

**Join key:** `home_team_name + snapshot_date`. The home team name is parsed from the event name (e.g., "Golden State Warriors at Minnesota Timberwolves" extracts "Minnesota Timberwolves" as the home team).

In hindsight, making this more robust by totalling the home and away teams' W/L data could add even more information not captured by a solo team's record. If the OKC Thunder are visiting the Utah Jazz, likely more Utahnians (right?) will flock to the Delta Center in Salt Lake City.

**Coverage:** Team records cover NBA, NFL, and MLB teams with a full season in the data window (Oct 2025 – Jan 2026). Minor league and international sports teams are not covered.

## Reddit Sentiment: Skipped

Reddit VADER sentiment was on the original plan. The hypothesis was that Reddit post volume and sentiment on artist subreddits or sports team subs would capture buzz that other signals miss.

It was deprioritized for two reasons. 

1) Reddit's API requires approved credentials with rate limits that would make full coverage across 6,800 slugs impractical
2) The signal quality is uncertain: Reddit communities have different sizes and engagement patterns that would require normalization before being useful, and there is no guarantee that subreddit activity correlates with ticket purchase intent rather than general fandom

The other three signals provide a reasonable test of the time-varying signal hypothesis without the credential and normalization overhead.

## Slug Compatibility Fix

This was an operational issue that would have silently produced zero matches if not caught.

The training data joins signals onto the main table using a normalized slug: an identifier like `artist::taylor_swift` or `artist::o_a_r`. The Python `_normalize()` function that generates these slugs strips all non-alphanumeric characters and replaces spaces with underscores:

```python
def _normalize(text):
    text = str(text).lower().strip()
    text = re.sub(r"[^a-z0-9\s]", "", text)   # strip ALL non-alphanumeric
    return re.sub(r"\s+", "_", text.strip())
```

When the signal caches were uploaded to BigQuery, the original SQL formula for slug generation used a different approach:

```sql
-- Old BigQuery formula (incorrect)
REGEXP_REPLACE(LOWER(event_name), r'[^a-z0-9]', '_')
```

The SQL formula replaces non-alphanumeric characters with underscores rather than stripping them. The result is that, for `O.A.R.`, Python produces `oar`, SQL produces `o_a_r_`. These two slugs didn't match. Any signal join on slug would silently return `NULL` for any artist with punctuation in their name.

**Fix:** The BigQuery expression was rewritten to mirror Python's behavior:

```sql
-- Corrected BigQuery formula
REGEXP_REPLACE(REGEXP_REPLACE(LOWER(event_name), r'[^a-z0-9\s]', ''), r'\s+', '_')
```

Verified against edge cases: O.A.R., Lady Gaga, Dave Matthews Band, and Hozier all produce identical slugs from both the Python and SQL formulas after the fix.

This kind of discrepancy is easy to introduce when scripts and SQL queries are developed independently. The only reliable test is to join on a sample and check coverage manually, where a zero-match join on a known entity is the signal.

Rows without a match receive `NULL` for those features. The model handles missing values with 0-imputation before scaling, which means the signal contributes nothing for unmatched rows rather than causing errors.

## Integration Decision

The original plan called for isolated ablation runs: baseline, +lastfm, +spotify, +team_records, and compare RMSE. After collecting and verifying all three caches, I decided against running isolated configs.

The reason is practical. Isolated ablations answer "does this signal help in isolation?" But the more useful question for a forecasting system is "do all of these signals together improve on the champion?" Running 60 Optuna trials per config × 5 configs × 2 stages (classifier + regressor) × 2 model families would require roughly 1,200 training runs. The compute cost is high and the isolated ablations would not reflect how the signals interact in a joint model.

Instead, all three social signals were uploaded to BigQuery alongside the other enrichment signals being added in Part 11 (news sentiment, Wikipedia hype, weather, inventory velocity, holiday features). The Part 11 sweep trains on all signals combined and compares directly against the Part 7 champion (18.53 RMSE). Post-hoc feature importance from the trained model answers which signals actually mattered.

Part 11 results show that `spotify_popularity` emerged as the highest-importance external signal (0.0206), while `lastfm_listeners` was lower but present (0.0097). Team records added coverage but did not resolve the Major Sports error problem.
