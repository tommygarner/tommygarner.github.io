Part 9 showed that Wikipedia entity embeddings do not improve ticket sales forecasting. The explanation was that tabular market features already encode identity: a $300 get-in price and 15 active listings in a 20,000-seat arena tells you more about likely demand than knowing an artist's career biography.

But there is a meaningful difference between two types of identity signal:

- **Static identity:** What this artist or team is (career history, genre, franchise market size)
- **Time-varying momentum:** How this artist or team is performing *right now* (streaming activity this week, current win streak, recent news coverage)

Wikipedia embeddings capture the first type. They are fixed at collection time and change nothing about the market. The second type is different. If a team has won 8 of its last 10 games, demand for their next home game is likely higher than it was two months ago when they were .500. If an artist's Spotify popularity jumped 15 points since their last tour, the demand curve for this run should look different than the model expects from historical patterns alone.

This post documents the collection of three time-varying signals fed into the Part 11 training sweep: Last.fm listener counts, Spotify artist popularity, and team W/L records.

## Last.fm Listener Counts

Last.fm exposes a monthly listener count for each artist through the `artist.getInfo` endpoint. This is different from the embeddings experiment. In Part 9, I used Last.fm bios and tags as text input for sentence transformers. Here the numeric listener count goes in directly as a feature.

The listener count measures current audience size, not historical fame. It updates as listeners add and remove plays. An artist with 2 million monthly listeners today is drawing more active attention than one with 200,000, regardless of Wikipedia page length or Grammy history.

Coverage: of the ~6,800 unique artist slugs attempted, about 2,875 matched (45.2% found rate). The gap reflects how slugs work: only named artists with Last.fm pages match. Broadway shows, comedy events, and sports games do not have Last.fm pages, so those rows receive `NULL` for this feature. Concert and Comedy events see the highest match rates.

## Spotify Popularity and Followers

Spotify returns two numeric signals per artist: `followers_total` (cumulative subscriber count) and `popularity` (a 0-100 score Spotify recomputes weekly based on recent streaming activity).

<img width="664" height="586" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f43fa0ae-547c-4353-af85-109d5bd23c66" />

*Figure 2: Spotify API architecture*

The `popularity` score is the more interesting of the two. Unlike follower count, which mostly reflects historical peak, popularity is recency-weighted. A breakout artist whose plays doubled in the past month scores higher than an established artist who has not released anything recently. This is the "hot right now" signal that follower count cannot capture.

I pulled signals for Concert and Comedy slugs only. Sports teams, Broadway shows, and festival events are not meaningful Spotify lookups.

Coverage came out to 92.8% found across ~7,408 rows, significantly higher than Last.fm because Spotify's search endpoint is more permissive and more major-label artists have pages.

One data quality issue: a `spotify_followers < 500` threshold was applied after collection. Some slug matches returned a coincidentally named act with a handful of followers rather than the intended touring artist. A slug like `artist::peach_bowl` could match a small local band rather than returning no result. Setting a floor at 500 followers marks those spurious matches as `NULL` rather than feeding incorrect signals into the model.

## Team Records: Point-in-Time W/L Data

For sports segments, the relevant signal is the team's current season standing at the time of the snapshot. A game between two teams with 60% win rates should see different demand than the same matchup when both teams are at 30%.

This has to be point-in-time. Using the final season record would leak future information into the training data. So I collected game logs from three league APIs:

- NBA: `nba_api` library, `TeamGameLog` endpoint
- NFL: `nfl_data_py` library
- MLB: MLB StatsAPI

<img width="1600" height="1109" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/d9ade3cf-6ed4-4f6e-9835-da2ba89cf810" />

*Figure 3: NBA API library*

For each team, the script builds a daily record table: wins, losses, win percentage, and current streak (positive = win streak, negative = losing streak) accumulated up to the snapshot date. The result is one row per team per date, joined on `home_team_name + snapshot_date`.

Coverage spans NBA, NFL, and MLB teams with a full season in the Oct 2025 to Jan 2026 data window. Minor league and international sports teams are not covered.

One thing worth noting: only the home team's record is used. A richer join would include both home and away team records, since matchup quality (a good team visiting) drives demand beyond just home team standing. That was left as a future improvement.

## Reddit Sentiment: Tested, No Signal

I built out the full Reddit collection pipeline (`scripts/fetch_reddit_signals.py`) with subreddit mapping for all buckets, VADER sentiment scoring, and a correlation mode that computes Spearman r against `sales_next_7d` before committing to a full training run. The initial "forward-only" concern in the script's docstring turned out to be overstated: changing `limit=100` to `limit=None` lets PRAW paginate back roughly 12 months, covering the full Oct 2025 to Jan 2026 training window. Taylor Swift on r/Music in the Oct 8-15 window returned 24 posts. The probe run hit 80% of Concert and Major Sports entities, confirming the mechanism works.

The correlation results stopped the experiment before model training. A first pass of 25 samples per bucket showed `post_count_7d` r=+0.435 for Concerts and r=+0.549 for Festivals, which looked promising. Expanding to 60 samples each collapsed both to near zero (r=+0.007 and r=-0.036). The early signal was small-sample noise from 6 and 8 covered rows respectively. The conclusion mirrors Part 9: the secondary market already captures the underlying popularity signal through get-in prices and active listing counts. Reddit post volume is an indirect and noisy proxy for what those features already encode directly. The script remains useful for live scoring in the Streamlit app, where Reddit buzz in the current 7-day window is a real-time signal unavailable during training.

## Slug Compatibility Fix

This was an operational issue that would have silently produced zero matches if not caught.

The training data joins signals onto the main table using a normalized slug. The Python `_normalize()` function strips all non-alphanumeric characters and replaces spaces with underscores:

```python
def _normalize(text):
    text = str(text).lower().strip()
    text = re.sub(r"[^a-z0-9\s]", "", text)   # strip ALL non-alphanumeric
    return re.sub(r"\s+", "_", text.strip())
```

When the signal caches were uploaded to BigQuery, the original SQL formula used a different approach:

```sql
-- Old BigQuery formula (incorrect)
REGEXP_REPLACE(LOWER(event_name), r'[^a-z0-9]', '_')
```

The SQL formula replaces non-alphanumeric characters with underscores rather than stripping them. For `O.A.R.`, Python produces `oar`, SQL produces `o_a_r_`. **These two slugs don't match, so any signal join would silently return `NULL` for any artist with punctuation in their name** — with no error, no warning, just missing data.

Fix: the BigQuery expression was rewritten to mirror Python's behavior:

```sql
-- Corrected BigQuery formula
REGEXP_REPLACE(REGEXP_REPLACE(LOWER(event_name), r'[^a-z0-9\s]', ''), r'\s+', '_')
```

Verified against edge cases: O.A.R., Lady Gaga, Dave Matthews Band, and Hozier all produce identical slugs from both formulas after the fix. The only reliable test for this kind of discrepancy is joining on a known entity and checking that coverage is non-zero.

## Why No Isolated Ablations

The original plan called for isolated ablation runs: baseline, +lastfm, +spotify, +team_records, compare RMSE, then decide. After collecting and verifying all three caches, I decided against it.

Isolated ablations answer "does this signal help in isolation?" The more useful question for a forecasting system is "do all of these signals together improve on the champion?" Running 60 Optuna trials per config across 5 configs, 2 stages, and 2 model families would require roughly 1,200 training runs. The compute cost is high, and isolated results would not reflect how the signals interact in a joint model anyway.

Instead, all three signals were uploaded to BigQuery alongside four additional enrichment sources being added in Part 11: news sentiment, Wikipedia hype, weather, and inventory velocity. The Part 11 sweep trains on all signals combined and compares directly against the Part 7 champion (18.53 RMSE). Post-hoc feature importance from the trained model answers which signals actually mattered.

The answer: **`spotify_popularity` emerged as the highest-importance external signal** at 0.0206 importance, with `lastfm_listeners` lower but present at 0.0097. Team records added coverage but did not resolve the Major Sports error problem. Those results are in Part 11.
