Day one of SXSW had me moving between mentor sessions and panels, all of them circling the same relevant industry questions: what does the music and entertainment industry actually want from data, and how do you build a career at that intersection? Four sessions with a surprising amount of overlap in their answers.

---

## Session 1: Mentor Session with Noel Morrison, SiriusXM / Pandora

Noel Morrison has spent 25 years in music and technology helping launch Beats Music and Apple Music before landing at SiriusXM/Pandora, where he leads product teams responsible for content supply chain, stations, and creator tools. In a 15-minute mentor session, he covered a lot of ground.

The first thing that struck me was how he framed what hiring managers are actually looking for on the data side of music tech right now: the ability to *build*, not just analyze. He recommended app-building for specific use cases, which got me excited about implementing Google's Agent Development Kit as a practical place to start. 

Noel also suggested that well-constructed A/B testing is an effective project that is currently used by product teams company-agnostic. For example, how should users communicate to personalized radios that they no longer want to listen to a song? He showed me what it looks like to have both analytical and product thinking at once when it comes to listening behavior. The best candidates, in his view, think like analysts and like product managers simultaneously.

His team's creator tools work was also exciting to learn from. The core problem they're solving is translating massive listener datasets into actionable, personalized insights for artists. So, not just "here's your stream count," but "here's exactly where your audience is clustering geographically and what that means for your next routing decision." As he put it: *give artists geographic-specific insights. If you have a super fan base in Michigan, maybe stop by there on your next tour.* 

The KPIs that matter in that context are trial-to-conversion and churn, and the product challenge is always about making data feel personal rather than transactional. 

He also flagged streaming fraud as a live problem the industry is actively working through on the analytics side. 

I'm super grateful for all he shared about the industry and made that 15 minute slot feel refreshing and impactful as my first impression to SXSW.

## Session 2: The Future of Festivals: Rock's Biggest Promoters

*Chamie McCurry (Danny Wimmer Presents), Chris Donohue (Front Gate Tickets / Ticketmaster), Darshan Kaler (Tradable Bits), Henryk Bik (Music Business Association)*

This panel reframed what festival data strategy actually looks like in practice. The executives on stage converged on a single idea: it truly is all about the fan experience. I got to learn how many different angles feom the festival side are using analytics to serve the fans better, and here's what I learned!

What I found most interesting was how the panelists described the shift from transactional ticketing to long-term fan relationship management. 
- Danny Wimmer Presents runs post-festival surveys that pull in tens of thousands of responses
- Front Gate layers in RFID entry data and wayfinding tools built on Apple Maps
- Tradable Bits stitches together engagement profiles that start building the moment someone buys a ticket. 

The goal at every step is converting a one-time buyer into what Darshan Kaler called a "forever fan," someone whose behavior has been shaped by presale access, side-stage perks, and meet-and-greet offers into something more emotionally lasting than a transaction. One stat that landed: **returning fans cost far less to retain than new ones cost to acquire**, which puts into context the entire analytics problem from acquisition metrics toward churn and loyalty signals.

I got to ask Darshan about a problem I think about a lot from my previous work at iHeartMedia and project ideas running through my brain: how do you stitch together online behavior (social engagement, streaming history) with offline behavior like ticket scans and merch purchases into a singl fan profile? 

His answer was blunt: it breaks down the moment the data pipeline isn't fully integrated and real-time. He described two parallel tracks that have to run simultaneously:

1) Optimizing the fan experience directly
2) Identifying changes that generate revenue

Darshan also said the tension between those two goals is where most of the interesting product decisions get made. The comparison he drew between sports teams (who own their venue and control the full data stack) and festival promoters (who are building every touchpoint and contingency planning from setup to teardown) was a super useful conversation I'll continue to think about as I attend more sessions and meet more people in the industry.

> *"Data without context isn't helpful. 100 different fans have 100 different experiences."*

## Session 3: From Streams to Strategy: The Label Services Evolution

*Panel · SXSW Music*
*Andrew Hove, Amanda McNeils (BMI), Matthew Aure (Bright Antenna Records), Victoria Tsigonis (FUGA)*

This panel put the distribution and services layer of the industry under the microscope. And truthfully, I don't know mchh about the Label and Distribution side of the industry, so it was great to be a sponge in this session.

Matthew Aure of Bright Antenna made the resourcing challenge plain: at a smaller label, you're managing physical product, sync licensing, and everything in between with limited headcount, so the partners you choose have to carry real weight. BMG's framing was different: with tools across radio, publishing, and more, the real question becomes what does the artist actually want, and how do you match that to what's available?

The data thread was just as sharp as the other sessions. FUGA's Victoria Tsigonis walked through a case study where a Brazilian producer's track blew up faster than anyone anticipated, so fast that the initial response was purely reactive. What FUGA did was come alongside the artist after the fact and use streaming spike data to pinpoint exactly where the momentum was concentrated, turning a lucky break into an actionable way to engage with a community. That story captures something the whole panel kept circling back to: **artists want data, they want it now**, and increasingly they understand it well enough that the best label partners are getting ahead of the numbers and bringing recommendations to artists *before* they've even logged into their dashboards.

On AI, the panel was measured about its usage and implenetation, stating it was useful for productivity and as a creative prompt, while genuinely valuable for affordable mastering, but still has a growing trust-and-safety problem as platforms get flooded with AI-generated tracks and partners spend more time explaining themselves to DSPs than doing actual creative work. It all starts with a human and ends with a human.

> *"Artists are looking for authenticity behind the data, not just what the numbers say, but why you should go to this area with this partner."*

## Session 4: Featured Session: The All American Rejects

*SXSW Featured Session*

I'll be honest, I truly stumbled into this one. I wasn't planning to attend a featured session with The All American Rejects. What I got was one of the most candid conversations of the day. 

They talked openly about a stretch touring with the Jonas Brothers: the shows were a well-oiled machine, but the band felt completely disconnected from it. The audiences weren't there for them, the energy was different, and by their own description it was miserable despite being objectively successful. That tension between visibility and passion was super honest.

The part that stuck with me analytically was a question they raised about reaching fans in markets that get overlooked: the smaller cities, the college towns, the metaphorical cornfields of Iowa where real fans exist but touring infrastructure doesn't always follow. It mapped directly to something I'd been thinking about: what if you could aggregate streaming and engagement data to surface those "house party" pockets of fans that algorithms and booking agents miss? 

They mentioned a University of Wisconsin–Madison show as an example of a bet that paid off. That's exactly the kind of application I'd want to build with Google ADK: an agent that pulls together geographic streaming clusters and flags underserved markets before the artist's team would ever think to look there.

The broader conversation tied into a thread across every session: **the brand now precedes the music**. That's not a complaint, it's a reality. If you don't have the follower counts and stream numbers, labels look through you. The artists who are thriving are the ones who figured out how to build the brand and the music at the same time.

> *"It's not bad, it's just different. People like a brand now. The music almost comes second."*

---

The thread connecting all four sessions was that data is only as useful as the relationship it's embedded in. Whether that's a streaming platform personalizing artist insights, a festival building a forever fan, a label partner getting ahead of a viral moment, or a band finding their people in a college town, **the analytics only matter when they're connected to something human** on both ends.
