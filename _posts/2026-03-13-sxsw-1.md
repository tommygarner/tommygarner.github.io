Day one felt like a full-speed introduction to how big and interconnected this industry really is. I went in expecting a few useful ideas, but I left realizing there are data applications everywhere, from product experiments to touring strategy.

---

## Session 1: Mentor Session with Noel Morrison, SiriusXM / Pandora

<img width="552" height="465" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f040e691-1309-41ef-9aa3-7da302a9c0fe" />

My mentor session with Noel Morrison was the best possible start to the week. He emphasized that music-tech teams are not just looking for people who can analyze dashboards; they want people who can build things that help artists and listeners make better decisions. His examples around A/B testing made this click for me, especially how small product changes can shape listening behavior in a measurable way.

The most practical takeaway for me was to think like both a product person and an analyst at the same time. That mindset shift made the whole week more useful because I stopped hearing session content as theory and started hearing it as project ideas I could actually execute.

---

## Session 2: From Streams to Strategy: The Label Services Evolution

![IMG_5846 (1)](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/f83a5809-ca4e-4248-b5c3-5afe09a80502)

This session helped me understand the label and distribution side much more clearly. I knew the terms before, but hearing people discuss what labels, distributors, and service partners each do in practice gave me a better mental map of how artists get supported (or slowed down) once momentum starts.

I also liked how direct the panel was about speed and context: artists do not just want numbers, they want interpretation they can use right now. That point stuck with me because it reinforced a theme I kept hearing all week: useful data is not just accurate, it is actionable.

---

## Session 3: The Future of Festivals: Rock's Biggest Promoters

![IMG_5847 (1)](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/955abd21-a400-4c06-9224-b6c8f299d6ec)

The festival panel made the "fan experience" conversation feel concrete instead of buzzword-heavy. The speakers talked through how promoters use survey responses, ticketing behavior, and on-site engagement to understand what keeps people coming back, and why retention is often more valuable than one-time acquisition.

What I appreciated most was how candid they were about integration challenges. It is easy to say "build a 360 fan profile," but hearing where that breaks in real workflows made me think harder about how data systems need to be stitched together before they can produce useful strategy.

---

## Session 4: Featured Session: The All American Rejects

I honestly stumbled into this session, and it ended up being one of the most memorable parts of day one. The band was honest about the gap between visible success and genuine connection, and that conversation made the "brand vs. music" tension feel very real.

It also connected back to my own curiosity about underserved markets. Their stories about fan pockets in places that are usually overlooked made me think about how better geographic signals could help artists make smarter routing bets.

![IMG_5866](https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/170a5f47-eac4-435d-aa13-4ab97a17a8da)

---

By the end of day one, I was seeing the same pattern across every room: the music business is full of different players, but they are all trying to answer similar questions about fans, trust, and timing.
